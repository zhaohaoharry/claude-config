**The rule.** In a paper's conclusion (and, more broadly, anywhere the paper's contribution is being characterized), do **not** write sentences that weaken, narrow, or hedge the paper's findings. Conclusions strengthen and generalize. Scope conditions are stated positively, as the configuration where the mechanism applies, not negatively, as places where it doesn't.

**Phrases to never write in a conclusion:**

- *"the mechanism's empirical reach is narrow"*
- *"the magnitudes, however, are local"*
- *"the result is specific to..."*
- *"these findings are modest..."*
- *"the contribution is limited to..."*
- *"applies to a small set of settings"*

These read as the author apologizing for their own paper. A referee or editor encountering them thinks "if even the author thinks the result is narrow, why should I take it seriously?"

**How to frame scope conditions positively.** When the mechanism requires specific institutional features, do not say so by listing what's missing in other settings. State the configuration where the mechanism applies and characterize it as broadly relevant.

**Bad (lists countries that DON'T have the configuration; reads as narrowing):**
> *"Australia's Murray–Darling already pairs a cap-and-trade SW market with a separate groundwater plan. The North China Plain has neither. The mechanism's empirical reach is therefore narrow."*

**Good (states where the mechanism applies; uses cases as evidence of relevance, not absence):**
> *"The mechanism applies wherever a buyer cluster without groundwater access trades surface-water entitlements with a seller cluster whose pumping cost lies below the trade price, and where the seller can replace the sold volume by pumping groundwater. This configuration is spreading as irrigation systems worldwide liberalize surface-water trade. Australia's Murray–Darling has pre-empted the leakage by pairing cap-and-trade with a separate groundwater plan; California's Kern and the broader American West, where surface-water market integration is advancing ahead of binding aquifer governance, are the cautionary cases where this paper's lessons apply directly."*

The "good" version still acknowledges that Murray–Darling avoided the problem, but frames it as evidence that the mechanism is real enough for policy to address it elsewhere — not as a reason the paper applies narrowly.

**Why this matters.** Conclusions are read by editors, referees, and policymakers as the author's own assessment of the paper's importance. A conclusion that hedges signals that the author doesn't believe in the result, which licenses the reader to discount it. The substance can be the same as in the body of the paper; the framing must be confident.

**This is not a license to overclaim.** The paper still says what it says. The conclusion does not invent results, does not generalize beyond what the model shows, does not promise external validity the design cannot deliver. But within the scope of what the paper has established, the conclusion frames the contribution as substantial and the mechanism as broadly relevant — not as a curiosity confined to one watershed.

Composes with [[feedback-no-research-note-language]] (don't telegraph structure) and [[feedback-no-decision-sentences]] (don't expose author's choices).

Applies across all research projects, especially in conclusions, abstracts, introductions, and any cover letter / response-to-editor language.
