In manuscript prose, **add explicit transition phrases between paragraphs** when the topic shifts. Without them, the writing reads as mechanical and sudden — each paragraph drops the reader into a new topic with no warning.

**Standard econ-paper flow markers:**

- After a section opening, introduce the first substantive paragraph with: *"I first look at..."* / *"I begin with..."* / *"Consider first..."* — names the next topic and motivates the order.
- Between paragraphs on related but distinct subtopics: *"I next examine..."* / *"I now consider..."* / *"Turning to..."*
- When introducing two or more exceptions or cases: *"Two cells deserve a brief explanation. Consider first..."* / *"The first exception arises..."* / *"Consider next..."* / *"A second exception arises..."*
- When shifting from the substantive lead (σ) to the secondary parameters: *"I next examine sensitivity to the other auxiliary parameters."* — explicit handoff.

**Bad (no transitions, mechanical):**
> *"I conduct sensitivity analysis on the auxiliary parameter values that enter the welfare comparison in Table 11.*
> *The six regulated cells are σ-invariant by construction. ..."*

**Good (transition signals the start of the σ analysis):**
> *"I conduct sensitivity analysis on the auxiliary parameter values that enter the welfare comparison in Table 11.*
> *I first look at σ. The six regulated cells are σ-invariant by construction. ..."*

**Why transitions are not "process narration":** the rule against author-decision sentences ([[feedback-no-decision-sentences]]) targets *meta-statements about choices* like *"I therefore treat X as the primary axis"*. Flow markers like *"I first look at..."* are not decisions; they are *signposts* that let the reader follow the order of argument. Top-5 econ papers use them routinely.

**Avoid colloquial transitions:** "So, ...", "Now, ...", "Well, ..." sound informal. Stick to econ-paper register.

Applies across all research projects.
