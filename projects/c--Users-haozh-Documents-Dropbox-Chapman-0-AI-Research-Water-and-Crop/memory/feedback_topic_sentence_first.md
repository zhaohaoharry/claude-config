In manuscript prose, the **first sentence of every paragraph is the topic sentence** and must carry the central meaning of the paragraph. For a result-introducing paragraph, the topic sentence is purpose + method in one move:

> "To examine X, I do Y."  (good)

NOT a soft warm-up or staged build-up like:

> "The accuracy of X can be examined by doing Y. I do Y..." (bad — drops the topic sentence behind a setup sentence)

**Why:** The reader scans first sentences. A paragraph whose lead sentence merely sets up the *next* sentence wastes that scan and reads as drafty. The verb of the paragraph — what I actually do — must appear in sentence one.

**How to apply:** When writing a paragraph that introduces an analysis or result:
- Sentence 1 = purpose + action: "To VERB X, I VERB Y."
- Sentence 2 = result location: "The results, reported in Table~Z, ..."
- Sentence 3 = supporting numbers.
- Sentence 4 = implication / what it justifies next.

This composes with [[feedback-econ-paper-flow]] (table-first → discussion → confirmation → next-stage estimates), which governs the **arc across paragraphs**. Topic-sentence-first governs the **internal structure within each paragraph**.

Applies across all research projects.
