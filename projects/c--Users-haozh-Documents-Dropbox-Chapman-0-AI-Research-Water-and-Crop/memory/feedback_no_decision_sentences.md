In manuscript prose, **do not state author decisions** as standalone sentences. Sentences of the form *"I therefore treat X as Y"* / *"I view X as central"* / *"I choose to focus on Z"* / *"I take the primary axis to be ..."* expose the author's selection process and add no information to the reader; the reader can infer what's central from what the section spends its time on.

**Good:** State the substantive claim, then move on. The implicit framing follows.

> *"This section examines the sensitivity of welfare comparisons to parameter values. I first look at σ, which captures the externality cost of over-pumping and is critical for welfare comparisons under open access and between open and regulated regimes."*
>
> (Sigma's primacy is implicit — it's the first parameter examined and the one whose role is most clearly described.)

**Bad:** *"I therefore treat σ as the primary axis of the sensitivity analysis."*

**Related rule:** also avoid "the sentence sets up the next sentence" patterns. If a sentence's only role is to explain why the *next* sentence is true (technical filler), drop it once the headline claim has been made. Example dropped sentence: *"This parameter scales the externality term in equation (welfare), and because each cell's welfare is linear in σ, the policy ranking can shift with σ in a way that no other input can induce."* — once we've already said σ is critical, this elaboration is unnecessary technical setup.

**Why:** These are "research-note language" patterns. They telegraph the author's reasoning instead of presenting the substance. Top-5 journals expect prose where every sentence carries new substantive content; meta-decisions and chain-of-thought belong in a response letter, not the paper.

Applies across all research projects.
