# Review of Economic Studies (REStud) — Style Guide

> **Companion skills (top-5 craft).** REStud is a top-5 journal, so the AEA-family workflow skills transfer: `aer-topic-selection`, `aer-identification` + `econometrics-playbook` (design), `aer-robustness`, `aer-introduction` (intro architecture), `aer-tables-figures`, `review-paper`, `aer-rebuttal`, `aer-replication`, `bib-validate`, `writing-deslop`. Those skills encode *AER* (AEA) house defaults — the 100-word abstract, AEA booktabs, AEA data policy apply literally only to AER (REStud is OUP / Review of Economic Studies Ltd). **For REStud the sections below override them**: keep its methodological/conceptual-innovation framing and its own house format.

### 1. Metadata
Journal: Review of Economic Studies (EN) / 经济研究评论 (中文, informal; no official Chinese name — the journal is English-only). Publisher: Oxford University Press on behalf of The Review of Economic Studies Ltd (a non-profit run by the economics profession; managed by a rotating board of Managing Editors, historically European-leaning, LSE/Oxford roots). Tier: top-5 general-interest economics journal (alongside AER, ECMA, JPE, QJE). Scope: economic theory, applied microeconomics, econometric methods/methodology, macro, development, political economy, environmental/spatial economics — explicitly broad across theory, applied, and methods. Distinctive reputation: rewards methodological and conceptual INNOVATION; historically the most junior-scholar-friendly of the top-5 (the annual 'REStud Tour'/May Meeting showcases new PhDs). Language: English (British or American spelling both accepted; British forms common). Last updated: 2026-05. Sources consulted: 4 PUBLISHED OUP-typeset exemplars (McGuirk & Nunn 2025 92(1):404-441; Cruz & Rossi-Hansberg 2024 91(2):899-939; Casey 2024 91(1):192-228; Alesina-Miano-Stantcheva 2022 advance-access rdac011) plus 1 PRE-PUBLICATION working-paper manuscript (Donna & Espin-Sanchez, Dec 2023). House-style claims (masthead, section numbering, editor footnote) are verified ONLY from the four typeset PDFs.

### 2. What this journal rewards
The editorial north star is a CLEAR, GENERALIZABLE ADVANCE — most often methodological or conceptual — that the profession will reuse. Winning papers either (a) build a new model/framework offered as a 'workhorse' (Cruz & Rossi-Hansberg's spatial integrated-assessment model; Casey's directed-technical-change correction), or (b) identify a precise, ethnographically/theoretically-motivated MECHANISM and nail it with falsification tests (McGuirk & Nunn; Alesina et al.'s survey-experiment design). A reduced-form 'we found effect X' without a transferable method, a model correction, or a sharply identified mechanism is not enough. REStud is more tolerant of single-authored theory and of younger authors than the other top-5, but the bar on rigor and on positioning against the named frontier is identical.

### 3. Positioning & framing
Openings are QUESTION-DRIVEN. Three patterns observed: (1) a stack of motivating questions then 'In this paper, we propose/quantify...' (Cruz: 'What will be the impact...? How will individuals react...?'); (2) a one-line stakes sentence narrowing to a specific empirical question framed as open ('Whether this mechanism results systematically in violent conflict is an empirical question' — McGuirk & Nunn); (3) the abstract itself opens with the research question ('Does immigration change support for redistribution?' — Alesina et al.). The contribution is then articulated EXPLICITLY and usually ENUMERATED ('Our contributions are, first... Second... Third...'; 'we make three main contributions: (1)... (2)... (3)...'). A 'fit' question is one where the answer requires a new method, a model, or a cleanly-identified mechanism — not merely a new dataset applied to a settled method. Always position against a NAMED frontier paper you build on or depart from (Desmet et al. 2018; Hassler et al. 2021a; Bordalo et al. 2016).

### 4. Structure conventions
REStud section structure is LOOSE (typical of English top-5) — there is NO fixed skeleton; order follows the argument. Verified HOUSE STYLE from typeset PDFs: top-level headings are Arabic-numeral + PERIOD + ALL CAPS ('1. INTRODUCTION', '2. THE MODEL', '8. CONCLUSIONS'); subsections are numeral.numeral + period + sentence-case (often italic) title ('2.1. Transhumant pastoralism'). Observed top-level orders: theory/quant paper = Intro / Model / Quantification(or Calibration) / Results / Adaptation-or-Mechanisms / Policy / Conclusion (Cruz: 7 sections; Casey: 6). Empirical paper = Intro / Background-and-Context / Data / Cross-sectional-or-Descriptive / Main-estimation / Mechanism-tests / Implications-and-Policy / Conclusion (McGuirk & Nunn: 8 sections; Alesina et al.: ~6). Lengths: introduction is LONG (4-7 pages, ~3,000-4,500 words); conclusion is short-to-medium (3-6 paragraphs). Heavy use of Online Appendix / Supplementary Materials for derivations, robustness, data. A 'roadmap' paragraph ('The rest of the paper is organized as follows...') is standard and ends with 'Section N concludes.' Interrogative section titles are allowed (Alesina §4 'WHERE DO MISPERCEPTIONS COME FROM?').

### 5. Abstract conventions
One paragraph, ~150-250 words (observed: 190-250). NO equations, NO mathematical notation, NO citations, NO section references. Required content: (1) the research question or the modeling/methodological contribution stated up front; (2) data/design or model class; (3) headline results with CONCRETE magnitudes (e.g. '20% losses in parts of Africa', 'cumulative energy use...12% higher', 'welfare increased by 23.4 real pesetas per farmer per tree'); (4) for empirical papers, a one-line policy or mechanism takeaway. Avoid hedging filler, avoid listing robustness checks, avoid 'this paper'. Followed immediately by a 'Key words:' line and a 'JEL codes:' line.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The identification/method bar is HIGH and varies by genre but is uniformly demanding. Applied micro: a credible causal design with explicit, falsifiable predictions and multiple falsification/placebo tests is expected — referees reliably demand that you rule out alternative mechanisms (McGuirk & Nunn run 5+ such tests: timing within year, temperature-vs-rainfall, mobile-not-pastoral vs pastoral-not-mobile, location on agricultural land). Survey/experimental: pre-registration (AEA RCT Registry), IRB approval, attention checks, incentive-compatibility checks (Alesina et al.). Structural/quantitative theory: model inversion, structural estimation of key functions, explicit counterfactuals, and an argument (often the Lucas critique) for why the structural approach beats reduced-form (Cruz). Pure theory: tractable model, analytic results, then quantitative calibration to data with a clear correction of a prior literature shortcut (Casey). Across all: voluminous online appendix with derivations and robustness is effectively mandatory.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
VARIATION is real here — capture it. Three observed placements: (a) fully WOVEN into the introduction with no separate section (Cruz; McGuirk & Nunn); (b) a RUN-IN italic heading 'Related Literature.' inside the intro (Casey); (c) a numbered SUBSECTION '1.1. Related literature' inside the intro (Alesina et al.); the working-paper Donna draft uses a standalone 'Related Literature' heading at the intro's end. A standalone numbered top-level literature section is NOT the norm. Citation format: author-year, parenthetical, semicolon-separated, '&'-free in narrative ('Miguel et al. 2004; Solow 2013; Burke et al. 2015'); 'et al.' for 3+ authors. Cite the NAMED frontier you build on/depart from and the methods sources; density is high (intros cite 40-80+ works). Reference list style is OUP/REStud author-year (the working-paper exemplar used a numbered-bracket list, which is a draft artifact, not REStud style).

### 8. Language & register
Register: formal but direct and readable; the prose narrates the argument, not just results. Person: first-person plural 'we' for co-authored, first-person singular 'I' fully acceptable for sole-authored (Casey). Hedging: measured ('suggestive evidence', 'consistent with the hypothesis') but conclusions are stated plainly. Jargon: technical terms used freely but defined on first use (e.g. 'transhumance', 'final-use vs primary energy', 'backfire'). Formatting: NO boldface in running text; NO bullet lists in the main body (enumeration done as 'first... second... third...' in prose); equations displayed and numbered in the body but NEVER in the abstract. Spelling: British OR American both seen across papers — pick one and be consistent within the paper. Section titles in ALL CAPS per house style; never end a heading with a period EXCEPT the mandated numeral period ('1. INTRODUCTION').

### 9. Policy framing
Policy implications are present and welcomed but SUBORDINATE to the scientific contribution — REStud is not a policy journal, so policy is a consequence of the analysis, not its selling point. Placement: a dedicated late section or subsection for policy-relevant papers (Cruz §6 on carbon taxes/subsidies; McGuirk & Nunn §7 on aid/conservation/political power) AND a policy-implications paragraph in the conclusion. There is NO national-strategy-alignment expectation (this is a global, non-partisan journal — unlike Chinese flagship journals). Institutional/contextual background IS expected: empirical papers carry a substantial 'Background and Context' section (McGuirk & Nunn §2 on the herder-farmer symbiosis; Donna §2 on the 700-year Mula water institution). Frame policy as 'our estimates imply' / 'the findings suggest', and note when policies do NOT work (a credible negative policy result is valued, e.g. aid projects 'tend not to mitigate the effects').

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
Fast-no signals specific to REStud: (1) no methodological, conceptual, or identification advance — a competent reduced-form result with no transferable contribution; (2) abstract containing equations, or failing to state a concrete contribution/magnitude; (3) introduction that does not position against the NAMED frontier the journal's referees know, or that omits the explicit contribution statement; (4) a structural/quantitative claim made without the supporting online appendix (derivations, robustness, identification arguments) — REStud referees expect to verify; (5) an empirical mechanism asserted but not subjected to falsification/placebo tests; (6) experimental work without pre-registration/IRB; (7) ignoring the Lucas critique when using estimated relationships for counterfactuals; (8) cosmetic house-style violations signaling unfamiliarity (no 'Key words'/'JEL codes' block, wrong section-heading scheme, boldface or bullets in body) — not fatal alone but a negative signal.

### 11. Before/after example
GENERIC: 'This paper studies the relationship between rainfall and conflict in Africa. Using a large dataset of rainfall and conflict events, we run regressions and find that lower rainfall is associated with more conflict. Our results have important policy implications for managing climate change in developing countries.' REStud-STYLE (modeled on McGuirk & Nunn's actual framing): 'Whether climate change systematically triggers violent conflict is an empirical question, and the channel matters for policy. We propose a specific, ethnographically-motivated mechanism: in low-rainfall years, transhumant pastoralists migrate to neighbouring farmland before the harvest, disrupting a centuries-old symbiosis and igniting conflict. Combining ethnographic data on traditional pastoral and agricultural territories with high-resolution rainfall and geocoded conflict data (1989-2018), we find that a one-standard-deviation rainfall decline in a cell’s transhumant-pastoral nearest neighbour raises local conflict risk by about 24%, while the same shock to a non-pastoral neighbour has a negligible effect. We confirm the mechanism through five falsifiable predictions—on the location, seasonal timing, phytomass channel, and the mobile-and-pastoral interaction—and show that aid projects do not mitigate it whereas pastoral political representation does.'

### 12. Checklist
1. Does the abstract state a methodological/conceptual contribution (not just a finding) and report concrete magnitudes, with NO equations? 2. Does the introduction open with a research question or a stack of questions, then 'In this paper, we...'? 3. Is the contribution stated EXPLICITLY and enumerated ('first... second... third...' or '(1)...(2)...(3)...')? 4. Does the intro position the paper against at least one NAMED frontier paper it builds on or departs from? 5. Are top-level section headings 'N. ALL-CAPS TITLE' (numeral+period+caps) and subsections 'N.N. Sentence-case title'? 6. Is there a roadmap paragraph ending '...Section N concludes.'? 7. Is the literature woven into the intro or in a 1.1/run-in subsection (NOT a standalone numbered top-level section)? 8. For empirical work: are there multiple falsification/placebo tests ruling out alternative mechanisms? 9. For structural/quantitative work: is there a Lucas-critique-style justification for the structural approach and an online appendix with derivations + robustness? 10. For experiments: are pre-registration (AEA RCT Registry) and IRB approval reported? 11. Are 'Key words:' and 'JEL codes:' lines present under the abstract? 12. Is the running text free of boldface and bullet lists, with enumeration done in prose? 13. Is person consistent ('we' for co-authored, 'I' acceptable for sole-authored) and spelling consistent (British or American throughout)? 14. Does the conclusion restate the contribution, give a policy/mechanism takeaway, and include an explicit limitations or 'future work' paragraph? 15. Are policy implications framed as consequences of the analysis ('our estimates imply') rather than as the paper's primary selling point?
