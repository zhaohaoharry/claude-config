# Journal of Political Economy (JPE) — Style Guide

> **Companion skills (top-5 craft).** JPE is a top-5 journal, so the AEA-family workflow skills transfer: `aer-topic-selection`, `aer-identification` + `econometrics-playbook` (design), `aer-robustness`, `aer-introduction` (intro architecture), `aer-tables-figures`, `review-paper`, `aer-rebuttal`, `aer-replication`, `bib-validate`, `writing-deslop`. Those skills encode *AER* house defaults — AER's 100-word abstract, AEA booktabs, AEA data policy apply literally only to AER. **For JPE the sections below override them**: keep JPE's price-theory/GE framing, ~90–110-word headingless abstract, and Roman-numeral sections.

### 1. Metadata
Journal: Journal of Political Economy (JPE) / 《政治经济学杂志》. Publisher: The University of Chicago Press, in association with the Department of Economics, University of Chicago (the 'Chicago school' / price-theory tradition). Tier: top-5 general-interest economics journal (alongside AER, ECTA, QJE, REStud). Scope: all fields of economics — micro and macro theory, applied micro, development, trade, IO, labor, economic history, public, environmental — with a house preference for work grounded in prices, markets, incentives, and general equilibrium. Editors rotate (recent handling editors seen on these papers: Rossi-Hansberg, Greenstone, Lochner, Kaplan). Language: English only; American spelling and usage. Last updated: 2026-05. Sources consulted: five recent JPE exemplars read in full front-to-back (abstract, full introduction, all section headings, conclusion): Nath 2025 (accepted ms); Burlig & Preonas 2024 (vol.132 no.9); Hornbeck & Rotemberg 2024 (vol.132 no.11); Alviarez, Cravino & Ramondo 2023 (NBER WP 27915 version of the JPE article); Farrokhi & Pellegrina 2023 (vol.131 no.9).

### 2. What this journal rewards
The editorial north star is a general, durable economic insight delivered with rigor — not a narrow estimate. JPE rewards papers that take a first-order economic question (cross-country income differences, the gains from a technology, climate adaptation, the value of infrastructure) and answer it by combining credible measurement with explicit economic structure, usually general equilibrium. The most JPE-ish move in this sample is the same in four of five papers: estimate a clean micro/reduced-form object, then map it into the parameters of a quantitative model to answer an aggregate counterfactual or welfare question. Price-theory sensibility is pervasive — wedges, markups, comparative advantage, relative prices, misallocation. The paper should leave the reader with a transportable lesson ('with great problems come great possibilities'; 'broadly-used technologies have larger impacts in distorted economies') plus reusable tools, not just a coefficient.

### 3. Positioning & framing
Openings are built as: (1) a one-to-two-sentence hook that is either a stark fact ('Nearly 1 billion people still lack electricity access'; 'two-thirds of every dollar spent on agricultural inputs ... are paid to foreign suppliers') or a big-picture economic puzzle ('Differences in income per-capita across countries are enormous'); (2) a sharpening of the gap or tension — often a conceptual complication a naive reader would miss (absolute vs comparative advantage; that social-savings is no longer an upper bound under distortions); (3) an explicit 'This paper...' / 'In this paper, we...' statement of what is done, frequently in two or three numbered moves; (4) a multi-paragraph guided tour of the findings WITH magnitudes pre-committed in the intro; (5) a scoping/caveat paragraph; (6) literature positioning woven in prose; (7) a one-line roadmap. A 'fit' question is causal-and-aggregate: it asks how some force shapes welfare, productivity, or allocation in equilibrium, and it can be answered by joining data to economic structure. Contributions are articulated as either a new measurement enabled by a new idea, a new general-equilibrium mechanism/tool, or a reconciliation of a divided literature — stated in plain economic language, not in econometric jargon.

### 4. Structure conventions
JPE top-5 structure is LOOSE — there is no fixed skeleton, and the section-numbering house style itself varies by production stage. PUBLISHED JPE articles use ROMAN numerals for sections and capital letters for subsections (I. Introduction, II. ..., A., B.) — confirmed in Burlig, Hornbeck, Farrokhi. ACCEPTED-MANUSCRIPT and working-paper versions use Arabic numerals (1, 2, 2.1, 2.1.1) — confirmed in Nath (accepted ms) and Alviarez (NBER WP). So adopt Roman numerals if writing for the published format. The introduction is long (5-8 pages) and is the single most load-bearing section; note that Hornbeck omits an 'Introduction' heading entirely (unlabeled intro, first heading is 'I. Data Construction'). Beyond the intro, order follows the argument, not a template. Typical empirical-then-structural arc: Introduction -> Institutional/Empirical Background -> Reduced-form Evidence -> Model -> Calibration/Estimation -> Counterfactuals -> (optional) Interpretation/Discussion -> Conclusion. Structural papers often split theory across two sections — a 'Stylized Model' for intuition then 'The Model' in full (Farrokhi) — and may add a standalone 'Discussion' or 'Interpretation' section (Hornbeck VI; Farrokhi V). Section counts in the sample: 6, 7, 7, 6, 8. Papers run long (35-90 manuscript pages including extensive appendices). Conclusions are short (1-2 pages), restate the insight, give the plausible range from robustness, and look forward.

### 5. Abstract conventions
One paragraph, no heading in the published version (a labeled 'ABSTRACT' with JEL codes appears only in NBER/working-paper versions). Target ~90-110 words for the published format (Burlig 102, Hornbeck 92, Farrokhi 101, Nath 99); working-paper abstracts can run longer (Alviarez NBER 148). Required content: the question/object, what the paper does ('We estimate...', 'We develop and estimate a new ... general equilibrium model...'), and the headline result — either a concrete number (Burlig's '33% internal rate of return'; Alviarez's 'roughly a third of the cross-country variance') or the qualitative welfare/distributional upshot (Farrokhi). First person — 'We' for coauthored, 'This paper' / 'I' for sole-authored (Nath). STRICTLY NO equations, no math symbols, no Greek letters, no citations, no section enumeration, no bullet points. Open with the hook (fact or puzzle), close on the takeaway. End several abstracts on a generalizable lesson rather than only a point estimate.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The identification/method bar is high and bimodal. Empirical papers need a credible research design carried at scale: Burlig combines a regression-discontinuity (a 300-person eligibility threshold) with a staggered difference-in-differences across India and rescales intent-to-treat effects to treatment-on-treated via IV, then does an explicit benefit-cost/IRR welfare calculation. Structural/quantitative papers need an explicit, well-specified general-equilibrium model whose key parameters are disciplined by data and whose counterfactuals answer the headline question (Nath, Hornbeck, Alviarez, Farrokhi all build or extend a quantitative model — Eaton-Kortum / quantitative spatial / development-accounting / Fréchet land-use). The signature JPE method is the hybrid: estimate a clean micro object, then 'map empirical estimates from micro-data directly into the parameters of quantitative models' to get equilibrium/welfare counterfactuals (Nath says this explicitly). Referees reliably demand: external validity / generalizability of the setting, a battery of robustness and alternative-parameterization checks (every paper has an 'Extensions and Robustness' subsection), transparent welfare interpretation (define 'welfare' operationally), and replication files. Standard errors, identification threats, and the endogeneity of the treatment (e.g., railroad construction) must be addressed head-on.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
No standalone 'Literature Review' section in any of the five. Related work is WOVEN INTO THE INTRODUCTION as prose, typically in a dedicated late-intro block flagged by an italic run-in lead — 'Related literature:' (Alviarez) or 'Related literature.—' (Farrokhi) — or simply as 'The paper builds on...' / 'More broadly...' paragraphs (Nath, Hornbeck). Citation density is high: dense parenthetical strings, sometimes 8-12 cites in one sentence (Hornbeck). Citation style is author-year, following the standard rule: spell out works with up to three authors in running text ('Costinot, Donaldson and Smith 2016'), and truncate four-or-more-author works to 'et al.' (the sampled papers do this routinely — Carleton et al. 2022, Gertler et al.). Mentions that look fully spelled out are simply three-author works. Cite the canonical foundations of your subfield (Fogel; Hsieh-Klenow; Eaton-Kortum; Arkolakis-Costinot-Rodriguez-Clare; Caselli development accounting) and position precisely against the 2-4 closest papers, stating exactly what you add. Footnotes carry substantial secondary citations and qualifications.

### 8. Language & register
Tone is confident, precise, and economically literate, with room for a distinctive authorial voice (memorable closing lines are tolerated: 'with great problems come great possibilities'). Person: first person throughout — 'we' (coauthored) or 'I' / 'this paper' (sole-authored). Hedging is calibrated and specific, not vacuous ('we can reject expenditure increases greater than 26%'), and scope caveats are stated openly in a dedicated paragraph. Jargon: price-theory and trade/macro vocabulary is expected (wedges, markups, comparative advantage, non-homotheticity, gains-from-trade formula) but key terms are defined operationally on first use ('By firm-embedded productivity we refer to...'; the welfare-definition footnote in Burlig). Formatting: no boldface in running text; NO equations or symbols in the abstract; display equations are numbered and used freely in the body (structural papers display the core equation early). Numbers in the intro are concrete (percentages, IRRs, shares) — pre-commit the headline magnitudes. Headings are title case and carry no terminal period.

### 9. Policy framing
Policy implications are present but disciplined and placed late — concentrated in the conclusion (and sometimes a dedicated paragraph at the end of the intro), not foregrounded over the economics. The pattern is an explicit enumerated list in the conclusion: Nath 'The paper has three primary policy implications. First... Second... Third...'; Burlig closes on targeting last-mile infrastructure; Farrokhi/Alviarez end on what the results imply for policy (closing firm-embedded-productivity gaps, distributional effects of globalization). There is NO national-strategy or government-agenda alignment expectation (this is a US/Chicago general-interest journal, not a national-policy outlet); implications are framed as general economic lessons and directions for future research. Institutional background IS expected when the setting is a specific program or country: Burlig devotes a full section (II) to the RGGVY program's history and mandate; Nath and Hornbeck supply historical/contextual grounding. Ground the policy claims in the model/estimates, and flag what the analysis omits.

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
Fast 'no' / desk-reject signals specific to JPE: (1) a narrow, purely descriptive, or local result with no general economic lesson or aggregate/welfare implication; (2) reduced-form estimates with no economic structure or interpretation when the question demands equilibrium reasoning (or, symmetrically, a model with no credible discipline from data); (3) weak identification — an empirical design that does not convincingly handle the obvious endogeneity (e.g., not addressing endogenous program placement); (4) no general-equilibrium / price-response thinking on a question where it clearly matters (partial-equilibrium answer to an equilibrium question); (5) an abstract or intro that hides the contribution, contains equations, or fails to pre-commit the headline magnitudes; (6) thin engagement with the canonical literature of the subfield, or failure to say precisely what is new relative to the 2-3 closest papers; (7) overclaiming beyond what the design/model supports, with no robustness battery and no replication materials; (8) a question that is faddish or small-bore rather than first-order for economics.

### 11. Before/after example
GENERIC (field-paper style): 'This paper studies the effect of rural electrification on village economic outcomes in India. Using a regression discontinuity based on a population eligibility cutoff, we find that electrification has small and statistically insignificant effects on per capita consumption. Our results contribute to the literature on infrastructure and development in low-income countries.' --- REWRITTEN TO FIT JPE (hook -> tension -> what we do -> magnitude -> general lesson): 'Nearly 1 billion people still lack access to electricity, and developing countries are investing billions of dollars in last-mile connections whose payoff is contested: seminal work finds large development gains, while recent village-level evidence finds electrification to be welfare-reducing. We show these discrepancies reflect scale. Exploiting a population-based eligibility threshold and a staggered rollout in India's RGGVY program — which reached over 400,000 villages — we estimate via regression discontinuity and difference-in-differences that the program meaningfully expanded access but generated limited economic impacts after three to five years. Rescaling to full electrification, we find a benefit-cost-negative 0% internal rate of return in small villages but a 33% return in large villages, where we see suggestive evidence of structural transformation. The broader lesson is that last-mile infrastructure, unlike first-mile, may be less likely to spur growth absent sufficient local scale.'

### 12. Checklist
1. Does the abstract run ~90-110 words, single paragraph, first person, with ZERO equations/symbols/citations, opening on a fact-or-puzzle hook and closing on the headline result or a general lesson? 2. Does the introduction pre-commit the key magnitudes (percentages, shares, IRRs) rather than deferring them to the results section? 3. Is there an explicit 'This paper... / In this paper, we...' contribution statement, ideally as 2-3 numbered moves or research questions? 4. Does the paper deliver a GENERAL, transportable economic insight (not just a local estimate) — statable in one sentence? 5. Is the question first-order and aggregate/welfare-relevant, with general-equilibrium or price-theory reasoning where it matters? 6. If empirical, is the identification design credible at scale and does it confront the obvious endogeneity head-on? If structural, are the model's key parameters disciplined by data and the counterfactuals tied to the headline question? 7. (Best-practice JPE move) Are clean micro/reduced-form estimates mapped into a model to answer an equilibrium or welfare counterfactual? 8. Is the related literature WOVEN into the intro (an italic 'Related literature' lead or 'builds on...' paragraphs), with the 2-4 closest papers positioned precisely — no standalone review section? 9. Are citations author-year (spell out works with ≤3 authors; 'et al.' for 4+), citing canonical foundations of the subfield? 10. If targeting the published format, are sections in ROMAN numerals with capital-letter subsections (I., II., A., B.) and headings free of terminal periods? 11. Is there a robustness/extensions section and a clear, operational definition of 'welfare' (and any contested terms)? 12. Are policy implications confined to the conclusion (often an enumerated First/Second/Third list), grounded in the results, with omissions/caveats stated? 13. Is the conclusion short (1-2 pages), restating the insight, the plausible range, and forward-looking tools/directions? 14. No boldface or bullet points in running text; American spelling; display equations numbered in the body only? 15. Are replication files / data provenance and the handling-editor acknowledgment in place?

*Grounding & confidence.* VERSION CAVEATS (important): Two of the five supplied PDFs are NOT the final published JPE typeset article, so front-matter conventions must be read carefully. (1) Nath (2025) is the JPE 'author's accepted manuscript without copyediting, formatting, or final corrections' (DOI 10.1086/734725); it uses Arabic section numbering and a labeled 'Abstract' header that will likely be re-typeset to Roman numerals in the final published version — its content/structure are reliable but its numbering style reflects the pre-typeset stage. (2) Alviarez, Cravino & Ramondo is the NBER Working Paper 27915 (Oct 2020) version of the article published in JPE 2023; it carries an NBER cover, JEL codes, a labeled ABSTRACT, and a longer (148-word) abstract — these are NBER conventions, not JPE house style, and the published JPE version will differ in front matter and likely in section numbering. Treat the published trio (Burlig, Hornbeck, Farrokhi) as the authoritative guide to JPE house FORMAT (Roman numerals, ~90-110-word headingless abstract), and treat all five as authoritative for FRAMING, structure-of-argument, and rhetorical conventions. Section-heading maps were generated by a regex scan of extracted PDF text and then spot-verified against the read pages; a few false positives from numeric table cells were discarded. Word/sentence counts for abstracts were computed on lightly de-mathified transcriptions (e.g., '%'->'percent') and are accurate to within a few words. The five papers skew toward trade/development/macro-with-GE and economic history; JPE also publishes pure theory, IO, labor, and finance, so the empirics-into-structure 'hybrid' pattern is dominant in THIS sample but is not the only JPE template. Claims about desk-reject signals, referee demands, and editorial 'north star' are reasoned inferences from the observed conventions plus general top-5 norms, not statements quoted from the PDFs, and should be presented as guidance rather than verified fact. All abstracts, full introductions, complete section heading lists, and conclusions were read directly from the PDFs; detailed results tables were intentionally skipped per instructions.

**Verifier flags (treat with caution):**
- [overstated] 8. JPE running-text citations spell out full author lists rather than truncating to 'et al.' (e.g., 'Costinot, Donaldson and Smith 2016', 'Lee, Miguel, and Wolfram 2020b'). — The two cited examples (both 3-author works) are spelled out verbatim, but all four papers routinely truncate 4+ author works to 'et al.' in body text: Nath 22x (Carleton et al. 2022, Hultgren et al. 2021), Burlig 13x (Gertler et al., Aklin et al.), Hornbeck 5x, Farrokhi 4x.
