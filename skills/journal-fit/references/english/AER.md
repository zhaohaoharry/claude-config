# American Economic Review (AER) — Style Guide

> **Companion skills (this is the home journal).** The AEA-family workflow skills apply to AER **directly and literally**: `aer-topic-selection` (contribution sizing + AER/AER:Insights/AEJ routing), `aer-identification` + `econometrics-playbook` (design), `aer-robustness` (robustness battery), `aer-introduction` (the 5-paragraph intro + the mandatory 100-word abstract), `aer-tables-figures` (AEA booktabs house style), `aer-submission` (pre-submit audit), `aer-rebuttal` (R&R), `aer-replication` (AEA Data & Code policy), plus `review-paper`, `bib-validate`, `writing-deslop`. This guide adds the house-style *fit* checklist; use `aer-workflow` to sequence the others.

### 1. Metadata
Journal: American Economic Review (AER) / 《美国经济评论》. Publisher: American Economic Association (AEA). Tier: top-5 general-interest economics journal; the AEA flagship. Scope: ALL fields of economics (micro, macro, trade, labor, IO, public, development, environmental, political economy, econometric method) plus shorter formats (Papers and Proceedings is separate). Language: English (US spelling). Last updated: 2026-05. Sources consulted: five recent AER articles/accepted manuscripts read in full intro + abstract + section map + conclusion — Rexer (Nigerian oil indigenization); Lyubich (people vs. places in carbon emissions); Barwick-Li-Waxman-Wu-Xia (urban transportation sorting, AER 2024 114(10)); Barwick-Li-Lin-Zou (From Fog to Smog, NBER WP 26541 of the AER 2024 article); Bombardini-Li-Trebbi (Did US Politicians Expect the China Shock?, AER 2023 113(1)).

### 2. What this journal rewards
The editorial north star is a GENERAL-INTEREST contribution: a clean answer to a question an economist in any field would find interesting, backed by either credible causal identification or a transparent structural/quantitative model. Papers get in by (1) posing a question broad enough that the abstract reads as important to non-specialists, (2) bringing novel data or a novel method to bear, and (3) delivering a small number of crisp, quantified headline results with welfare or policy stakes. AER rewards 'I am the first to show X' novelty claims that are defensible, and punishes incremental within-field exercises. Magnitudes matter: every paper here leads with numbers (a % of GDP, a benefit-cost ratio of an order of magnitude, a pp change in a voting probability).

### 3. Positioning & framing
Build the opening as a funnel: (1) one or two sentences on a broad real-world phenomenon or fact, usually with a marquee citation or a vivid anchor (Shell divesting $2bn from Nigeria; China's 2013 monitoring program; the China shock). (2) Narrow to the specific tension or unanswered question, often phrased as an explicit question ('Why does the state fail to protect these firms?'; 'Did US politicians expect the China shock?'). (3) State 'In this paper, I/we...' with the data and the method. (4) Preview the headline results WITH MAGNITUDES inside the intro, not deferred to results. (5) Articulate the contribution explicitly — four of five papers use a signposted block ('This paper makes several contributions' / 'We make three main contributions' with First/Second/Third), each item positioned against a named literature and ending with a defensible novelty claim. A 'fit' question is one whose answer changes how economists outside the subfield think, has a quantifiable stake, and can be credibly identified.

### 4. Structure conventions
AER is a LOOSE-structure journal: there is no mandated section skeleton, and length runs long (35-50 manuscript pages of body is normal; appendices are extensive). What is shared across papers is the FLOW, not fixed titles. Observed orders: (Rexer) Background -> Data -> Theory -> Empirical strategy -> Results -> Mechanisms -> Welfare -> Conclusion; (Transport) Policy Background+Data -> Model -> Estimation -> Counterfactual algorithm -> Counterfactual results -> Conclusion; (Bombardini) Empirical Model -> [methodology] -> Institutional Background+Data -> Results -> Counterfactuals -> Conclusion; (Fog) Intro -> Institutional Background+Data -> Awareness -> Empirical Framework (with an Identification + Identification-Threats subsection) -> Results -> Cost-Benefit -> Conclusion; (Lyubich) Intro -> Data+Stylized Facts -> Model -> Empirical Strategy -> Results -> Characterization -> Implications/Conclusion. Conventions: Introduction is always Section 1/I and is long (4-7 pages). Background+data are usually merged into one early section. The model or empirical framework typically PRECEDES the results; in method-led papers it can precede even the institutional background. There is NEVER a standalone 'Literature Review' section. A Conclusion is standard, though one author-typeset paper folded it into a final 'Implications' section. HOUSE NUMBERING in the published journal is Roman numerals for sections (I., II., III.) with capitalized titles and lettered subsections (A., B., C.); cross-references read 'Section VB'. Accepted manuscripts and the NBER working-paper versions often appear in Arabic (1, 1.1) before the journal re-typesets — so target Roman for a submission to match house style, but Arabic is tolerated at submission.

### 5. Abstract conventions
One paragraph, ~95-105 words (the five papers measured 96, 100, 100, 100, 103). NO equations or mathematical notation. NO citations. NO section references. Structure: one sentence of motivation/setting, one to two sentences naming the data and method/identification, then the results — and CLOSE on a headline quantified magnitude or a general-interest policy takeaway (e.g., '...at most between 2.3-5.7% of GDP'; '...benefits outweigh the costs by an order of magnitude'; '...reduce household carbon emissions by about 40 percent'). A JEL code block follows the abstract (and Rexer adds a Keywords line). Do not hedge; state findings declaratively.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The identification bar is high and explicit. Reduced-form papers must name a credible design (here: staggered-rollout DiD with testable orthogonality conditions; mover/two-way-fixed-effects design with a stated identifying assumption and a leave-out bias correction; difference-in-differences with event-study pre-trends, a placebo from aborted transactions, and modern heterogeneity-robust DiD diagnostics — Goodman-Bacon 2021, Callaway and Sant'Anna 2021). Structural papers must microfound preferences, address endogeneity/IV, and run welfare/counterfactual decompositions (the transport paper decomposes welfare along five margins; Bombardini uses moment-inequality estimation to handle partially observed information sets). Referees reliably demand: (1) the identifying assumption stated in words, not just symbols; (2) pre-trend/event-study evidence; (3) placebo and falsification tests; (4) a dedicated robustness battery (the Fog paper has a 4.3 'Potential Identification Threats' subsection; the transport intro previews IV-choice, measurement-error, and supply-elasticity checks); (5) translation of estimates into welfare or cost-benefit terms. Preview the identification strategy and the main robustness checks inside the introduction.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
NO separate literature-review section in any of the five papers — this is a firm AER convention. Related work is woven into (a) the opening funnel (one or two marquee citations to establish the phenomenon) and (b) a dedicated contribution block in the back third of the introduction, where each paragraph names a specific literature, summarizes it in one or two sentences, and states 'relative to X, we...' / 'this paper is the first to...'. Citation density is moderate-to-high in the intro and front sections, lighter in results. Cite the canonical anchors of each literature you touch (e.g., Autor-Dorn-Hanson for the China shock; Bayer-Ferreira-McMillan for sorting; Kline-Saggio-Solvsten for leave-out estimation) plus the two or three closest contemporary papers you must distinguish yourself from. Author-date format inline; full reference list headed REFERENCES at the end.

### 8. Language & register
Tone: confident, declarative, plain. Person: first-person — 'I' for sole-authored (Rexer, Lyubich), 'we' for multi-authored (the other three). Hedging is minimal and reserved for genuine caveats (external validity, partial-equilibrium approximation, 'we leave this for future research'). Jargon: field terms are used but key constructs are unpacked in prose (Lyubich explains the mover design via a two-household 'thought experiment'; the transport paper spells out the five welfare margins). Formatting: NO boldface in running text; NO bullet points in the body (enumerate with 'First,... Second,... Third,...' in prose); NO equations in the abstract. Display math is used freely in model/method sections. Numbered footnotes (not endnotes) carry asides, primary-source quotes, institutional detail, and small derivations. US spelling and AEA reference style.

### 9. Policy framing
Policy and welfare implications are integral, not appended. Every paper translates estimates into welfare, cost-benefit, or distributional terms (% of GDP; benefit-cost ratio; consumer surplus by income group; a counterfactual emissions reduction benchmarked against the IRA). Placement: results sections carry the quantitative welfare analysis (often a dedicated 'Welfare' or 'Cost-Benefit Analysis' section), and the conclusion draws the broader policy lesson and generalizes beyond the specific case. Institutional-background expectations are high: a full early section establishes the setting (the Nigerian oil sector and divestment; Beijing's three anti-congestion policies; China's monitoring rollout; the NTR/PNTR vote sequence). There is no national-strategy-alignment requirement (this is a US/international journal); instead, ground the policy relevance in welfare economics and external validity ('closer to a representative than a pathological case').

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
Fast 'no' signals: (1) a narrow, within-subfield question that would not interest a general reader — the single most common desk-reject reason at a top-5; (2) no credible identification strategy, or an identifying assumption never stated in words; (3) an abstract padded with equations, citations, or vague claims instead of a quantified result; (4) a standalone 'Literature Review' section (signals non-fit with house style); (5) results presented without magnitudes or without welfare/policy translation; (6) missing the expected robustness/placebo battery; (7) overclaiming novelty that a referee can puncture with one citation; (8) no institutional-background section so the reader cannot assess external validity. Length that is bloated without a matching contribution also draws editorial skepticism.

### 11. Before/after example
GENERIC (within-field, no stakes, no magnitude): 'This paper studies the effect of local firm ownership on oil production in Nigeria. Using firm-level data and a difference-in-differences design, we find that fields operated by domestic firms produce more oil than those operated by multinationals. We also examine the role of political connections and discuss implications for the literature on FDI.' AER-STYLE (general-interest framing, vivid anchor, quantified stakes, mechanism, welfare): 'Multinationals in the extractive sectors of weak states face resource theft by armed groups, yet the state often fails to protect them even though firms would pay for protection — a striking failure of Coasean bargaining. Studying Nigeria's oil indigenization, where local firms' onshore share rose from 6% to 40%, I show that despite lower operating quality local firms raise legal output by roughly 30% by cutting oil theft 54%, because political connections to military elites realign law-enforcement incentives. A bargaining model rationalizes this 'local advantage,' and the program's aggregate gains reach at most 2.3-5.7% of GDP.'

### 12. Checklist
1. Is the abstract a single paragraph of ~95-105 words with NO equations and NO citations, ending on a quantified magnitude or policy takeaway? 2. Does a JEL code block follow the abstract? 3. Does the introduction open with a broad, general-interest phenomenon and a vivid anchor or marquee citation before narrowing to the specific question? 4. Is the central question stated explicitly (ideally as a question) and broad enough to interest economists outside the subfield? 5. Are headline results quantified WITH MAGNITUDES inside the introduction, not deferred to the results section? 6. Is the identification strategy (or structural identification) previewed in the intro, with the key identifying assumption stated in words? 7. Is there a signposted contribution block ('This paper makes ... contributions' / 'First, Second, Third') positioning against named literatures with a defensible novelty claim? 8. Is there NO standalone 'Literature Review' section (related work woven into intro and front sections instead)? 9. Are citations author-date inline, with asides/detail in NUMBERED footnotes and a REFERENCES list at the end? 10. Is there an early institutional-background section establishing the setting for external validity? 11. Are estimates translated into welfare, cost-benefit, or distributional terms, with the broader policy lesson drawn in the conclusion? 12. Does the empirics include event-study/pre-trend evidence, placebo/falsification tests, and a robustness battery (previewed in the intro)? 13. Is the prose first-person ('I'/'we'), declarative, with NO boldface and NO bullet points in the body (enumerate in prose)? 14. For a journal-style submission, are sections numbered with Roman numerals (I., II.) and lettered subsections (A., B.), model/framework preceding results?
