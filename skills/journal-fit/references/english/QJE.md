# Quarterly Journal of Economics (QJE) — Style Guide

> **Companion skills (top-5 craft).** QJE is a top-5 journal, so the AEA-family workflow skills transfer: `aer-topic-selection` (is the contribution top-5-sized), `aer-identification` + `econometrics-playbook` (research design), `aer-robustness` (robustness/placebo/heterogeneity), `aer-introduction` (intro architecture), `aer-tables-figures` (tables/figures), `review-paper` (referee self-check), `aer-rebuttal` (R&R letters), `aer-replication` (data/code deposit), `bib-validate`, `writing-deslop`. Those skills encode *AER* house defaults — AER's 100-word abstract, AEA booktabs tables, AEA data policy apply literally only to AER. **For QJE the sections below override them**: keep QJE's fact-first framing, long Roman-numeral structure, and ~100–210-word abstract.

### 1. Metadata
- **Journal:** The Quarterly Journal of Economics (QJE). *Do not confuse with Peking
  University's 《经济学(季刊)》 (China Economic Quarterly) — a separate Chinese journal.*
- **Publisher / affiliation:** Oxford University Press on behalf of the President and
  Fellows of Harvard College; editorial home is the Harvard economics department.
- **Tier:** Top-5 general-interest journal (the "QJE" of AER/QJE/JPE/Econometrica/REStud);
  one of the oldest and most prestigious.
- **Scope:** All fields, with a strong revealed taste for big-picture empirical work,
  economic history, and public/labor/trade with credible identification, plus
  quantitative structural models tied to unusually good data.
- **Language:** American English.
- **Last updated:** 2026-05.
- **Sources consulted:** full PDFs of five exemplars — Shapiro (2021, 136(2), *published*),
  Heblich–Redding–Sturm (2020, 135(4), *published*), Cengiz–Dube–Lindner–Zipperer (2019,
  134(3), *published*), Exley–Kessler (NBER WP 26345 version of the 2022 paper),
  Kalyani–Bloom et al. ("Diffusion of New Technologies", INET WP 222 version). Structural
  claims rest on the three confirmed published versions (see Grounding note).

### 2. What this journal rewards
A single, durable, broadly-interesting result that changes how economists see something —
stated as a "fact" or a clean answer to a first-order question, not an incremental
coefficient. Winning papers either (i) document a new, surprising empirical regularity and
explain it (Shapiro: trade policy implicitly subsidizes carbon), (ii) answer a long-debated
policy question more credibly than anyone before (Cengiz et al.: the minimum wage and total
low-wage jobs), or (iii) build a quantitative model disciplined by exceptional data to
settle a structural question (Heblich et al.: railways and the modern metropolis). QJE
rewards ambition and generality; the result must matter to non-specialists and survive
heavy refereeing (papers routinely thank "four anonymous referees").

### 3. Positioning & framing
The opening funnel is wide-to-narrow and **fact-first, not gap-first**. Sentence one states
a real-world phenomenon, a live policy debate, or a striking scene (Heblich: the London
Underground's 3.5 million daily journeys; Cengiz: "$15/hour" campaigns; Shapiro literally
opens "This article describes a new fact, then analyzes its causes and consequences"). The
contribution is then signaled bluntly — "In this article/paper, we…" — and, where
warranted, with a plain-language priority claim (Shapiro frames the fact as one "previous
literature has not tested for or identified"). A "fit" question is first-order and
general-interest, phrased so a non-specialist cares ("what is the overall effect of minimum
wages on low-wage jobs?"), and the paper promises a credible, quantified answer. Headline
magnitudes appear in the intro itself, benchmarked against a familiar number. The intro
usually previews results section-by-section and includes an explicit caveats paragraph.

### 4. Structure conventions
QJE imposes no rigid template, but published structure is **regular**: top-level sections
are Roman-numeral, ALL CAPS, beginning **I. INTRODUCTION**; the closing section is usually
CONCLUSIONS (Cengiz titles theirs **V. DISCUSSION** — a permitted variant). Subsections are
lettered (II.A, II.B, …). The canonical empirical order across the exemplars:

> I Introduction → (II Historical/Institutional Background, when the setting matters) →
> Data → Econometrics/Methodology → Results → Mechanisms/Explanations →
> Counterfactuals/Consequences → Conclusions.

Theory-heavy papers insert a Theoretical Framework section before the quantitative analysis
(Heblich V). **There is no standalone literature-review section** — related work is folded
into the introduction. Papers are long (~50–75 journal pages); proofs, extra robustness,
and data detail go to an **Online Appendix**. Introductions run ~5–10 printed pages and end
with a one-paragraph roadmap. (The Chinese near-fixed skeleton — 引言→文献综述→…→政策建议 —
does **not** apply here.)

### 5. Abstract conventions
One paragraph; no headings, no citations, no equations, no displayed math, no footnotes.
Length ~100–210 words across the exemplars (Heblich ~114, Shapiro ~148, Cengiz ~210). Lead
with the question or headline fact, name the data and method in a clause or two, then state
the main result(s) **with concrete magnitudes** (51.5%; $550–$800 billion/year; +2.8% with
the implied null). Multi-result papers enumerate "First/Second/Third" inside the single
paragraph. Close with the broader implication. JEL codes follow the abstract. Avoid:
methodological hedging, literature positioning, and any "organized as follows" material.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The credibility bar is high and **foregrounded in the introduction**, not deferred.
Reduced-form papers need a clearly named, defensible design — DiD/event-study with explicit
parallel-trends and falsification tests (Cengiz: pre-trends, placebo upper-tail bins,
Meer–West reconciliation), or a "natural experiment" from a sharp technological/
institutional break (Heblich: the steam railway). Structural papers must discipline the
model with real data, state identifying assumptions in plain language, and benchmark
estimated elasticities against the prior literature (Heblich's 0.086 vs the 3–8% survey
range). Referees reliably demand: confrontation of competing prior estimates, extensive
robustness (often a dedicated section + appendix), an exogeneity/instrument defense, and a
candid caveats paragraph. New-measurement papers must validate the measure. Magnitudes and
standard errors belong in the intro text.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
No separate review section. Related work lives in the introduction as labeled paragraphs —
"This article builds on several literatures"; "We contribute to three related literatures.
First… Second… Third…" — each closing with an explicit "we contribute by / differ from /
are the first to" statement that positions the paper against its **2–4 closest competitors
by name** (Cengiz vs Meer–West 2016; Exley–Kessler vs Reuben et al. 2014 and Charness et al.
2018). Citation density in these paragraphs is high. Cite the canonical foundational works
plus the most recent closely-related papers, and **differentiate sharply from the nearest
rival** rather than listing. Format: author–date in running text; substantive elaboration,
asides, and institutional/legal detail go in **numbered footnotes** (not endnotes).

### 8. Language & register
Confident and direct, but carefully hedged on causality. Person: first-person — "I"
(sole-authored, e.g., Shapiro), "we" (co-authored); active voice ("I find", "we show", "we
document"). Plain declarative sentences, technical but accessible to a general audience;
define field-specific terms on first use. Hedging is reserved for causal/welfare claims and
model assumptions, **not** for the headline fact. Conventions observed: no boldface in
running text; no equations in the abstract; displayed equations confined to methods/theory;
key magnitudes as actual numbers, never "large"/"near-zero"; enumerated points as "First, …
Second, …" prose, **not** bulleted lists.

### 9. Policy framing
Policy relevance is the motivation but rarely a normative prescription. Papers motivate from
a live policy debate in the opening (minimum-wage campaigns; carbon tariffs) and devote a
bounded passage — often one labeled intro paragraph plus a conclusion paragraph — to
implications framed in welfare/efficiency terms (Shapiro's first-best/second-best
discussion), not advocacy. Institutional/historical background is taken seriously and gets
its own early section when it matters. **There is no expectation of alignment with any
national strategy or development plan** (unlike the Chinese journals); the audience is
global and the framing is positive-economics-first, with policy offered cautiously.

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
1. The question is narrow or field-internal — no general-interest reader would care.
2. The contribution is incremental ("one more coefficient" on a settled question) rather
   than a new fact or a decisive answer.
3. Identification is weak or merely asserted — no credible design, no parallel-trends/
   falsification, no defense against the obvious confounder.
4. The abstract hedges, cites, contains equations, or buries the result.
5. The intro opens "There is a growing literature on…" (gap-first) instead of a real-world
   phenomenon or first-order question.
6. Magnitudes are vague ("a large effect") instead of benchmarked numbers.
7. A separate literature-review section — signals the author hasn't internalized house style.
8. No engagement with the closest competing paper; conflicting prior estimates left
   unreconciled.
9. Thin robustness, no Online Appendix; or a paper too short/underbuilt for the long-form,
   heavily-refereed format.

### 11. Before/after example (contribution / opening paragraph)
**Generic (won't land):** "This paper studies the relationship between trade policy and the
environment. There is a growing literature on carbon leakage and border tax adjustments, but
the political economy of how existing tariffs map into emissions has received little
attention. Using input–output data and tariff schedules, we run regressions and find a
statistically significant negative association between tariff levels and industry emissions
intensity, contributing to the literatures on trade and on environmental policy."

**QJE-style:** "This article describes a new fact, then analyzes its causes and
consequences: in most countries, import tariffs and nontariff barriers are substantially
lower on dirty than on clean industries. This difference creates a global implicit subsidy
to CO₂ emissions in traded goods — on the order of $550 to $800 billion a year, larger than
direct global fossil-fuel subsidies — and so contributes to climate change. I show the
pattern holds in most countries and in cooperative and noncooperative tariffs, trace it to
the greater protection of clean downstream industries, and explain it with a lobbying model
in which firms seek low tariffs on inputs while final consumers are unorganized."

*(Fact-first; a named magnitude benchmarked to a familiar number; mechanism previewed —
versus the generic gap-first, vague-result framing.)*

### 12. Checklist
1. Does the abstract lead with the question/fact and a quantified headline result, in one
   paragraph, no citations, no equations (~100–200 words)?
2. Does sentence one of the intro state a real-world phenomenon, live policy debate, or new
   fact — **not** a literature gap?
3. Is the contribution stated explicitly ("In this article we…") and, where warranted, with
   a priority/enumerated First/Second/Third framing?
4. Are the main magnitudes given as actual numbers in the introduction, benchmarked against
   a familiar comparison?
5. Is the identification strategy named and defended in the intro, with the closest
   competing estimate confronted by name?
6. Is related work woven into the introduction as "we contribute to / differ from"
   paragraphs, with **no** separate literature-review section?
7. Are top-level sections Roman-numeral ALL CAPS (I. INTRODUCTION …) with lettered
   subsections, and is there an early Background section if the setting matters?
8. Does the intro end with a one-paragraph roadmap?
9. Are citations author–date in text, with asides/institutional detail in numbered
   footnotes (not endnotes)?
10. Is there an explicit caveats paragraph and a robustness section, with proofs/extra
    results in an Online Appendix?
11. Is person consistent (I / we), active-voice, with no boldface in running text and no
    bulleted lists in the body?
12. Is policy framed positively (welfare terms) and bounded, with no national-strategy
    alignment shoehorned in?
13. Would a general-interest economist find the question first-order and the answer
    surprising or decisive?
14. Is the paper long-form enough (substantial intro, multiple results, mechanisms) for
    QJE's format rather than a short note?

general top-5 knowledge — well-grounded inference, not a published QJE editorial statement.
Guidance is calibrated to QJE's empirical/quantitative majority; pure-theory pieces may
differ. Refresh after ~1 year against recent issues.
