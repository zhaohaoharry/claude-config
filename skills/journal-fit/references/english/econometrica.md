# Econometrica — Style Guide

> **Companion skills (top-5 craft).** Econometrica is a top-5 journal, so the AEA-family workflow skills transfer: `aer-topic-selection`, `aer-identification` + `econometrics-playbook` (design), `aer-robustness`, `aer-introduction` (intro architecture), `aer-tables-figures`, `review-paper`, `aer-rebuttal`, `aer-replication`, `bib-validate`, `writing-deslop`. Those skills encode *AER* (AEA) house defaults that do **not** apply to Econometrica (Econometric Society / Wiley): the 100-word abstract, AEA booktabs, and AEA data policy are AER-specific. **For Econometrica the sections below override them**: keep its theorem-proof formalism, Supplemental-Material proof placement, and lighter general-interest framing — and note the empirical 5-paragraph intro formula is only a loose fit for pure-theory papers.

### 1. Metadata
Journal: Econometrica (EN) / 计量经济学 (中文，直译；学界多直接称"Econometrica"). Publisher/affiliation: published by Wiley on behalf of the Econometric Society; flagship journal of the Econometric Society. Tier: top-5 economics journal; the most formal/technical of the top-5. Scope: economic theory, econometric theory, and quantitative/structural empirical work where the contribution is a method, model, or identification result of general interest; the exemplars here skew applied quantitative trade and structural IO. Language: English. Last updated: 2026-05. Sources consulted: 5 exemplar PDFs (Oberfield-Raval 2021 and Kopytov et al. 2024 are typeset published Econometrica articles; Allen-Atkin, Farrokhi-Lashkaripour, Hsiao are working-paper versions of Econometrica papers — see grounding_notes), plus general knowledge of Econometrica pure-theory house norms.

### 2. What this journal rewards
The editorial north star is a GENERALIZABLE methodological or theoretical advance, demonstrated rigorously. Econometrica rewards papers whose central object is a new model, a new identification/estimation result, or a new analytical characterization — the empirical application exists to showcase the method, not the reverse. A paper gets in when a referee can name the transferable contribution in one sentence ("a dual-decomposition method for GE optimal policy"; "recovering the aggregate elasticity from micro cross-sections"; "endogenous networks under uncertainty with provably efficient equilibrium"). Rigor is non-negotiable: results are stated as numbered Propositions/Theorems with proofs. Cleverness of mechanism and tractability of the framework are prized over breadth of policy relevance.

### 3. Positioning & framing
Openings move from a concrete, named problem to a precisely stated obstacle, then to the paper's resolution. Pattern observed in all five: (1) one or two sentences of motivation (a macro fact, a policy failure, a managerial survey, a NYT quote); (2) a sharp statement of the technical or conceptual difficulty, often attributed to prior work by name and year (e.g., the Diamond-McFadden-Rodriguez non-identification result; the free-riding problem; Hulten's theorem); (3) a pivot 'In this paper, we...' / 'I develop...' that states the contribution as a sequence of action verbs (we construct / derive / prove / characterize / estimate); (4) a results preview with concrete numbers; (5) a literature block and a section-numbered roadmap. A 'fit' research question is one whose answer requires a new method or yields a clean theoretical characterization of general interest — not merely a new estimate for a new dataset. Naming devices help (Proposal 1/Proposal 2 in Farrokhi; coordination problem/commitment problem in Hsiao; first-moment/second-moment in Allen-Atkin) and recur as the paper's organizing spine.

### 4. Structure conventions
Section order is LOOSE by top-5 convention (no fixed skeleton), but two dominant templates appear. THEORY-FIRST (Oberfield-Raval, Kopytov, Farrokhi): 1 Introduction; 2 Model/Theoretical Framework (with numbered subsections building the environment); 3+ Characterization sections organized around Propositions/Lemmas; then Quantification/Calibration; then Conclusion. EMPIRICS-FIRST / STRUCTURAL-IO (Allen-Atkin, Hsiao): 1 Introduction; 2 Background/Empirical context; 3 Data; reduced-form Stylized Facts OR Demand/Supply model blocks (each Model/Estimation/Estimates); Counterfactuals/Quantification; Conclusion. Typical length 6-7 top-level sections plus heavy appendices. Roadmap sentences are embedded in the intro, not in a separate paragraph. Conclusions are short (0.5-1.5 pages): recap + one paragraph of extensions/limitations, never new results. CRITICAL Econometrica-specific norm: the published main text often contains ONLY statements of formal results, with proofs and most derivations relegated to a Supplemental Material or a proofs appendix — practice varies (Kopytov has zero proofs in the body; Oberfield-Raval keeps an Appendix A of proofs but pushes Appendices B-G to the Supplement). Build the paper assuming a Supplemental Material companion.

### 5. Abstract conventions
One paragraph, ~130-175 words (exemplars: 132, 133, 137, 144, 172). Prose only — NO equations, NO displayed math, NO symbols beyond the occasional inline unit. Required content: (a) the problem/question; (b) the method or model as the lead contribution ('We construct a model...', 'I develop a framework...', 'We develop a framework to estimate...'); (c) one or two HEADLINE NUMERIC results stated concretely (0.5-0.7 elasticity; 3.4%; 33-68%; 7.4 Gt; $15/ton) — published Econometrica abstracts favor specific magnitudes; (d) what the result rules in or out. Avoid: citations, equations, vague 'near-X' phrasing (use the actual number), and listing every robustness check. A KEYWORDS line follows the abstract in the typeset version.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The bar is a clearly identified, transferable method or result. For THEORY: a complete model, numbered Assumptions, and results stated as Lemmas/Propositions/Theorems with proofs (in body or Supplement); existence/uniqueness/efficiency results are valued (Kopytov proves a unique efficient equilibrium). For STRUCTURAL/QUANTITATIVE: a model that yields tractable, transparent estimating equations; identification argued verbally AND formally — referees reliably demand (i) explicit instruments with an economic exclusion-restriction story (Oberfield's amenity/Bartik/shift-share instruments for labor supply; Allen-Atkin's price-elsewhere and yield variation; Hsiao's weather shocks as supply/demand shifters), (ii) a stated source of identifying variation, (iii) over-identification or robustness across specifications, and (iv) computational tractability of the estimator (Hsiao's Euler/CCP 'estimate without solving the model'; Farrokhi's dual decomposition avoiding GE elasticities). Quantitative papers must map model objects to named data sources and report units. Referees expect a Supplemental Material with full proofs, derivations, data construction, and robustness.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
WOVEN-IN is the norm. Four of five exemplars integrate prior work into intro paragraphs ("Our work is related to a large literature...", "This paper relates to a number of strands..."); only Farrokhi uses a labeled 'Related Literature' subsection at the end of the intro, organized First/Second/Third/Lastly with an explicit marginal-contribution sentence per strand. There is NO standalone numbered 'Literature Review' section anywhere in the body. Citation density is high in the intro and footnotes, light in the technical sections. Cite: the foundational result you are overcoming (by author-year, e.g., Houthakker 1955, Diamond-McFadden-Rodriguez 1978, Hulten 1978), the 2-4 closest contemporary papers (and state precisely how you differ), and the methodological lineage (Hopenhayn 1992, Hotz-Miller 1993, Scott 2013 for dynamic estimation; Acemoglu-Azar 2020 for networks). Econometrica bibliography style: full-caps author surnames in the reference list, with bracketed back-references to citing pages. 'et al.' for 3+ authors in running text.

### 8. Language & register
Tone: terse, precise, impersonal-technical. Person: first-person plural 'we' for multi-author, first-person singular 'I' for sole-author (Hsiao uses 'I' 406 times) — both standard. Hedging is minimal and specific ('largely ineffective', 'declined slightly') rather than throat-clearing. Jargon: field-standard terms used without apology; new objects are defined formally (DEFINITION 1) before use. Formatting discipline: no boldface in running prose; no bullet lists in the main text body (numbered conceptual items like 'Proposal 1/Proposal 2' or '(1)...(2)...' challenges are allowed inline); equations are displayed and numbered; NO equations in the abstract. Theorem environments (ASSUMPTION/LEMMA/PROPOSITION/THEOREM/COROLLARY/DEFINITION) are set in small-caps or italic headers and numbered sequentially. Section headings are short title-case noun phrases with no terminal period.

### 9. Policy framing
Policy is present as MOTIVATION and as an INTERPRETATION of results — rarely as advocacy (some conclusions do make brief normative climate-policy statements) and never as a standalone numbered policy-recommendation section. Even the three climate/trade exemplars keep policy implications inside the Conclusion or a 'Discussions' section (Farrokhi's Section 6) and phrase them as research directions or quantified trade-offs, not prescriptions ('Future work could explore distributional considerations...'; 'collecting firm-level emissions data offers a promising research direction'). Institutional/background detail is included only to the extent it justifies modeling choices (Hsiao's palm-oil Background section motivates the demand/supply structure; Allen-Atkin's Empirical context motivates the Pareto trade-cost assumption). No national-strategy alignment, no normative framing. The reward is for what the method reveals about the policy mechanism, not for endorsing a policy.

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
Fast-no signals specific to Econometrica: (1) no transferable method or theorem — 'just another estimate' for a new dataset reads as a field-journal paper; (2) results asserted without proof, or a model with no formal characterization (no Assumptions/Propositions); (3) identification hand-waved — no instrument, no exclusion restriction, no stated identifying variation; (4) equations or heavy notation in the abstract; (5) a model too stylized to take to data when the claim is quantitative, or too ad hoc to yield clean results when the claim is theoretical; (6) missing Supplemental Material with proofs/derivations; (7) over-claimed general-interest framing unsupported by the technical content; (8) sprawling, unfocused contribution (referee cannot name the one advance); (9) policy advocacy in place of analysis. Desk-reject risk also rises for prose that violates house form (bulleted main text, boldface emphasis, missing numbered results).

### 11. Before/after example
GENERIC (field-journal flavor): "We study how trade affects farmers in India. Using data on crop prices and a trade-cost measure, we run regressions showing that lower trade costs change what farmers grow. We find that trade has both benefits and costs for farmers, and we discuss policy implications for agricultural development." ECONOMETRICA-STYLE (rewritten): "While trade liberalization raises average returns through specialization, it also alters the volatility of returns by weakening the negative correlation between local prices and local productivity shocks. When production is risky, producers are risk averse, and insurance markets are incomplete — as for farmers in developing countries — this second moment of the gains from trade carries first-order welfare consequences. In this paper, we explore these second-moment effects empirically, analytically, and quantitatively. Using forty years of Indian agricultural micro-data, we document three stylized facts; we then embed a portfolio-choice problem into a many-location, many-good general-equilibrium Ricardian model that yields tractable estimating equations for trade costs and risk preferences; finally, we structurally estimate the model to quantify the welfare effects of India's highway expansion." The rewrite (i) leads with a precise economic mechanism, (ii) states the obstacle, (iii) pivots to a sequenced contribution culminating in a transferable framework, and (iv) drops the vague 'policy implications' gesture.

### 12. Checklist
1. Does the abstract lead with the method/framework as the contribution and report at least one concrete headline number, with zero equations and <=175 words? 2. Does the introduction state the technical/conceptual obstacle precisely, attributing it to prior work by author-year? 3. Is the contribution articulated as a sequence of action verbs (construct/derive/prove/characterize/estimate) that each map to a later section or numbered result? 4. Are the central results stated as numbered Assumptions/Lemmas/Propositions/Theorems (not merely asserted in prose)? 5. Are proofs and full derivations present, and is there a Supplemental Material companion housing them (with most or all proofs out of the body)? 6. For empirical claims, are instruments, the exclusion restriction, and the identifying variation stated explicitly in words? 7. Is the literature woven into the introduction (or a single end-of-intro subsection) with a stated marginal contribution per strand, and no standalone numbered review section? 8. Is the roadmap embedded in the intro with section-number pointers? 9. Is the conclusion short (<=1.5 pages), recapping results plus one paragraph of extensions, with no new results and no policy advocacy? 10. Are policy implications confined to motivation and a discussion/conclusion paragraph, phrased as trade-offs or research directions? 11. Is the prose free of boldface emphasis and bulleted main-text lists, with displayed/numbered equations and no math in the abstract? 12. Do section headings lack terminal periods and read as short title-case noun phrases? 13. Is the person consistent (we for coauthored, I for sole-authored) and the tone terse and unhedged? 14. Can a referee name the single transferable advance in one sentence?

**Verifier flags (treat with caution):**
- [overstated] Econometrica abstracts are single-paragraph prose of roughly 130-175 words with no equations and at least one concrete numeric result. — Measured word counts: Oberfield 144, Kopytov 138, Farrokhi 146, Hsiao 156, Allen-Atkin 204. All single-paragraph and equation-free (verified). But four of five counts differ from the offered numbers, Allen-Atkin (204, the NBER w22276 version provided) breaks the '130-175' range, and Allen-Atkin's abstract reports no concrete numeric result (only qualitative findings plus 'forty years' of data) - so the word range and the 'at least one numeric result' rule are both contradicted by an exemplar.
- [overstated] In published Econometrica articles, all proofs can be relegated entirely to the Supplemental Material, leaving the main-text body with only statements of formal results. — True for Kopytov (2024): main body runs Sec 1-10 with zero Proof/Appendix headers; its 5 'proof' mentions are inline pointers and all proofs sit in Supplemental Appendices (2024a/2024b). But the other published exemplar, Oberfield-Raval (2021), contains a main-text 'APPENDIX A: PROOFS OF PROPOSITIONS' with actual PROOF environments ('The proofs of all propositions are contained in Appendix A'). So this is one permitted option demonstrated by n=1, not a property of published Econometrica articles generally; the co-cited paper does the opposite.
- [overstated] Conclusions are short (about half a page to one and a half pages), recapping results and offering a single paragraph of extensions or limitations, with no policy advocacy. — Length and recap-plus-extensions structure verified: Oberfield-Raval ~2/3 page ending on a skill-premia/inequality extension; Kopytov ~0.5 page ending on international/firm-level/dynamic extensions; Farrokhi ~12 lines 'broader implications'; Hsiao ~18-line reflective close. But 'no policy advocacy' is contradicted: Hsiao's conclusion makes normative endorsements ('Green trade policy will be an important tool for protecting the vast forests...'; 'international climate action will be crucial for meeting our global climate targets'), and Farrokhi notes omitted factors 'justify policies such as green technology subsidies.' The structural part holds; the blanket 'no policy advocacy' does not.
