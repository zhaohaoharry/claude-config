# Per-Journal Style Guide — Schema

Every file under `references/english/` and `references/chinese/` follows this exact
12-section template, so guides are comparable and the skill can apply them
uniformly. Cross-cutting norms shared by every journal in a language family live in
`_common-english-top.md` / `_common-chinese-top.md`; a per-journal file records only
what is **distinctive** about that journal, and may say "see common" where it simply
inherits a shared norm.

## Authoring rules
- **Ground every non-obvious claim** in evidence — a recent (≤5 yr) paper, the
  journal's author guidelines, or an editorial statement — and list it in §1 sources.
- **Be concrete and falsifiable** ("abstracts run ~100–150 words, one paragraph, no
  citations"), never generic ("write clearly").
- **Conventions, not a cage.** Frame everything as norms to be aware of, not a
  template to force prose into. The skill must never flatten a draft into a formula.
- **Tight.** The whole file should be skimmable in ~2 minutes.
- **Date-stamp it** (§1 last-updated). These conventions drift; guides need refresh.

## Template (copy this into every journal file)

### 1. Metadata
- **Journal:** English name / 中文名

- **Tier / standing:**
- **Scope:**
- **Language:**
- **Last updated:** YYYY-MM
- **Sources consulted:** papers (with years), author guidelines, editorials

### 2. What this journal rewards
3–5 lines — the editorial north star: the kind of contribution that gets in.

### 3. Positioning & framing
How the opening is built; how the contribution is articulated; what counts as a
"fit" research question for *this* journal.

### 4. Structure conventions
Section order and length expectations. English top-5 are usually loose — say so.
Chinese journals often follow a near-fixed skeleton — lay it out explicitly.

### 5. Abstract conventions
Length, required content, what to avoid.

### 6. Methods & identification expectations
The bar a paper must clear; expected methods; what referees reliably demand.

### 7. Literature & citation norms
Separate review section vs woven-in; citation density; who/what to cite
(recent journal-self cites; policy documents for Chinese journals).

### 8. Language & register
Tone, person, hedging, jargon level, formatting rules (boldface, equations in
abstract, display vs inline math, etc.).

### 9. Policy framing
Degree and placement of policy implications; national-strategy alignment;
institutional-background expectations. Heavy for 经济研究 / 管理世界; light for
经济学（季刊）and most English journals — say where (and whether) it belongs.

### 10. Common rejection / desk-reject triggers
The fast "no" signals specific to this journal.

### 11. Before/after example
One short paragraph (an intro hook or a contribution statement) shown "generic,"
then "rewritten to fit this journal." The single most useful section — make it real.

### 12. Checklist
8–15 actionable items the skill runs a draft against, each a yes/no it can check and
comment on. This is what makes the guide *operational* rather than merely descriptive.
