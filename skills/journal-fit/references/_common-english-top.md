# Common Norms — English Top-General Journals (AER · QJE · JPE · Econometrica · REStud)

Cross-cutting conventions shared by the English top-5 general-interest journals. The
per-journal files record what is **distinctive**; this file is the shared baseline.
**Last updated:** 2026-05.

## What they share
All five reward a **novel, general-interest contribution** that a broad economics
audience cares about — not an incremental result on a narrow question. A paper must
(a) pose a question that matters, (b) make a credible (well-identified or well-proved)
advance, and (c) state precisely what is new relative to the closest existing work.

## Structure — LOOSE, not a fixed template
Economics does **not** use the IMRaD format or a standalone "Literature Review"
section common in other fields. A typical empirical paper runs:
1. Introduction (long — 4–10 pp; does most of the persuading)
2. Setting / institutional background / conceptual framework or model
3. Data
4. Empirical strategy / identification
5. Results
6. Mechanisms / heterogeneity / robustness
7. Conclusion (short)
   plus an **Online Appendix** (proofs, robustness, extra tables — often longer than
   the paper itself).

Theory papers: model → assumptions → propositions/results → proofs (often appendix) →
applications or quantification. Order is flexible; sections are named for their
**content**, not by a generic template.

## The introduction — your most valuable page budget
The modern econ intro is a tight funnel, usually in this order:
1. Motivating question or real-world puzzle — a hook grounded in the world, **not** in
   "a gap in the literature."
2. Why it is hard / why the answer is not obvious.
3. "In this paper, we…" — what you do (setting, data, design) in 1–2 paragraphs.
4. "We find that…" — the headline results, **with numbers, early** (often p. 2–3).
5. Identification / empirical-strategy paragraph — why the estimates are credible.
6. Contribution & relation to the literature — 2–4 paragraphs placing the paper against
   the **closest 3–6 papers**, saying exactly what is new ("We differ from X by…").
7. Roadmap — one short paragraph (increasingly optional).

Do not bury the findings. Referees form a view in the first three pages.

## Abstract
~100–150 words, **one paragraph, no citations, no equations, no section references.**
State the question, the data/method or setting, and the headline finding **with the key
magnitude.** Plain and declarative. (Global rules: no equations in abstracts; use actual
numbers, never "near-nominal.")

## Contribution & literature
- Literature is **woven into the introduction** (sometimes a short "related literature"
  subsection), never a standalone front-matter survey.
- Cite the closest work precisely and generously; situate, don't dismiss.
- **Author–date citations** in text (Author, Year); footnotes for asides; no endnotes.
- Cite recent same-tier work where genuinely relevant.

## Identification / rigor culture
- **Empirical:** a credible research design is the price of entry — RCT, natural
  experiment, IV/shift-share, RD, DiD (with modern robust estimators and parallel-trends
  evidence), or a structural model. Pre-empt the obvious threats **in** the paper.
- **Theory:** clean setup, explicit assumptions, proved propositions, intuition before
  algebra, and a worked application or quantification.
- Show robustness; acknowledge limitations honestly.

## Register & format
- First-person plural ("we"), active voice; present tense for what the paper does and
  for results.
- Intuition before formalism; define notation; minimize jargon and acronyms.
- **No boldface in running text.** "et al." for 3+ authors in running text.
- Tables and figures are self-contained with full notes (AER/RES format).
- Section titles describe content ("The China Shock and Local Labor Markets"), not
  generic labels ("Empirical Results").

## Title
Short and substantive. Common patterns: a question ("Is Free Trade Good for the
Environment?") or "Topic: Evidence from <setting>" ("Migrants and Firms: Evidence from
China"). Avoid vague, buzzword-stacked, or colon-heavy titles.

## Length & polish
40–60 typeset pages plus an online appendix is typical. The prose is expected to be
clean on submission — sloppy writing reads as sloppy thinking to referees.
